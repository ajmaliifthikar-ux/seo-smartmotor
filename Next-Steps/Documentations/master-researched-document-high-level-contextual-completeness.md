### 5C — Psychographic Customer Segments

**Segment 1: "The Status-Conscious Professional" (Emirati / Arab Executive)**

- Car: Mercedes S-Class, GLE, BMW 7 Series, Porsche Cayenne
- Age: 35–50
- Primary fear: Workshop damaging their prestige vehicle or using non-OEM parts without telling them
- Primary desire: White-glove experience that matches the car's status; they want to feel their car is being respected
- Switch trigger: A single incident of deception on parts or an unprofessional reception experience
- Word-of-mouth trigger: A premium lounge, a complimentary detail upon collection, a follow-up call — these become stories shared at majlis gatherings[^13]
- Willingness to pay: High; will pay 20–30% premium for confidence and prestige experience

**Segment 2: "The Practical Expat Family" (Western / Arab Expat, 30–45)**

- Car: Audi Q5/Q7, BMW X3/X5, Volvo XC90, VW Tiguan
- Age: 30–45
- Primary fear: Being overcharged or misled on unnecessary repairs; having the car unavailable for family logistics
- Primary desire: Transparency, fast turnaround, clear communication (English), convenience (pickup/delivery)
- Switch trigger: Any unresolved complaint, or a workshop that communicates better (WhatsApp updates)
- Word-of-mouth trigger: Pickup and delivery service; finding out the bill is exactly what was quoted[^13]
- Willingness to pay: Medium-high; price-aware but not price-first

**Segment 3: "The Car Enthusiast / Performance Buyer"**

- Car: BMW M Series, Audi RS, Porsche 911, Porsche Macan
- Age: 28–42
- Primary fear: Mechanics who don't understand performance specs or use wrong fluids/settings
- Primary desire: Technicians who speak their language — track performance, upgraded specs, correct torque values
- Switch trigger: Evidence the technician actually understands their model (specific certification, model knowledge)
- Word-of-mouth trigger: Instagram-worthy garage content, post-service performance confirmation data, attending car meets organized by the workshop
- Willingness to pay: Very high for the right expertise; low tolerance for generalists

**Segment 4: "The Fleet Manager / Corporate Decision-Maker"**

- Cars: Mixed European fleet — Mercedes, BMW, Audi (executive fleet for companies, hotels, developers)
- Age: 35–55
- Primary fear: Workshop downtime causing business disruption; inconsistent quality across vehicles
- Primary desire: Single invoice, service contract, guaranteed turnaround times, account management
- Switch trigger: One missed deadline or an overinflated invoice without prior approval
- Word-of-mouth trigger: SmartMotor-branded fleet reports, proactive maintenance scheduling; they want a "managed fleet partner," not a mechanic
- Willingness to pay: Medium; negotiates on volume

**Segment 5: "The Budget-Savvy Second-Hand Buyer" (South Asian Expat, Mid-income)**

- Car: Used Mercedes C-Class, BMW 3 Series (5–8 years old), Audi A4
- Age: 28–40
- Primary fear: Getting cheated — paying for parts that weren't replaced, being told they need repairs they don't
- Primary desire: Honest assessment, fair price, genuine parts confirmation
- Switch trigger: Discovering a cheaper alternative with the same quality signal
- Word-of-mouth trigger: Being saved from an unnecessary repair; sharing the honest experience in their community WhatsApp group
- Willingness to pay: Low–medium; very community-driven discovery (South Asian expat networks are tight)

## 5. Recommended Legal / Policy Pages for SmartMotor.ae (UAE Context)

You are operating a UAE‑based digital service and collecting user data (contact forms, bookings, analytics). The UAE has a modern data protection framework (PDPL – Federal Decree‑Law No. 45 of 2021), consumer protection law (Federal Law No. 15 of 2020), and an e‑commerce / electronic transactions regime (Federal Decree‑Law No. 46 of 2021) that together regulate online services and consumer rights. [web:35][web:38][web:40][web:43]

**Strong recommendation:** work with a UAE‑qualified lawyer to draft and localise these pages. The notes below help you brief them and structure the content; they are not legal advice.

### 5.1 Privacy Policy (PDPL‑Aligned)

The UAE’s PDPL requires clear information on how you collect, use, store and share personal data and the rights of data subjects (access, correction, deletion, consent withdrawal, etc.). [web:40][web:43]

**Suggested sections for your Privacy Policy page:**

1. **Introduction & scope**  
   - State that the policy applies to all visitors and customers of smartmotor.ae and to data collected via website forms, WhatsApp links, call tracking, and analytics.

2. **What data you collect**  
   - Contact details (name, phone, email), vehicle information, booking details, and technical data (IP, browser, cookies).

3. **Legal basis & purpose of processing**  
   - Explain that you process data to respond to inquiries, provide services, manage bookings, send service reminders, and improve your website, in line with PDPL principles of consent, purpose limitation and minimal collection. [web:40][web:43]

4. **Data sharing & international transfers**  
   - Identify any third‑party tools used (hosting, email marketing, CRM, analytics) and whether data leaves the UAE, describing safeguards where applicable. [web:40]

5. **Data security measures**  
   - Describe technical and organisational measures to protect personal data against unauthorised access, loss or misuse, aligned with UAE government guidance on privacy and security. [web:34][web:37][web:40]

6. **Data subject rights & contact details**  
   - Explain the right to access, correct, delete, restrict, or object to processing, and provide a dedicated email/contact for privacy requests (e.g., privacy@smartmotor.ae or sales@smartmotor.ae). [web:40][web:43]

7. **Retention periods**  
   - Outline how long you keep contact/booking records and when they are anonymised or deleted.

8. **Cookies & tracking technologies**  
   - Briefly describe cookies and link to a more detailed Cookie Notice if you use advanced analytics/marketing tools.

9. **Updates to the policy**  
   - Reserve the right to update, with the last‑updated date clearly visible.

### 5.2 Terms & Conditions (Service and Website Use)

Under UAE consumer and e‑commerce laws, online traders must clearly set out key terms, prices, limitations, cancellation and refund rules, and must avoid “harmful terms” that unfairly restrict consumer rights. [web:35][web:38][web:41]

**Suggested sections for Terms & Conditions:**

1. **Definitions & scope**  
   - Define “Smart Motor Auto Repair”, “Customer”, “Website”, “Services”, etc.

2. **Booking & service process**  
   - Explain how customers can book online / by phone, how estimates are provided, and when a work order is considered accepted.

3. **Pricing, estimates & payments**  
   - Clarify that estimates may change if additional faults are found, but you will always seek customer approval; state accepted payment methods (cash, card, bank transfer, BNPL partners like Tabby). [web:1]

4. **Warranties & liability**  
   - Describe labour warranty (e.g., 6‑month labour warranty on repairs) and limitations, ensuring compliance with UAE consumer protection principles and avoiding clauses that waive essential rights or create “harmful terms.” [web:1][web:35][web:41]

5. **Customer obligations**  
   - Responsibilities such as providing accurate vehicle information, picking up vehicles promptly, and removing valuables.

6. **Cancellations, no‑shows & storage fees**  
   - Set reasonable rules for missed appointments and storage charges, ensuring they are not disproportionate penalties under the Consumer Protection Law. [web:35][web:41]

7. **Website acceptable use & IP**  
   - Basic rules for using content, logos, and images; prohibiting misuse or scraping.

8. **Governing law & jurisdiction**  
   - State that UAE law (and, if appropriate, the courts of Abu Dhabi) govern disputes.

### 5.3 Cookie Notice

If you use analytics, advertising pixels or remarketing tools, UAE data protection guidance recommends transparency about cookies and tracking. [web:37][web:40][web:43]

# Typography Guidelines & Font Usage

This document outlines how typography should be implemented across different development environments (Web, Flutter, etc.) for Smart Motor, ensuring brand consistency.

## 1. Core Brand Fonts
Our typography stack relies on clean, modern, and highly legible fonts that support both English (LTR) and Arabic (RTL) natively.

### Primary English Font Stack
- **Inter** (Primary Sans-Serif)
- **Segoe UI** (Windows Native Fallback)
- **Roboto** (Android Native Fallback)
- **San Francisco / -apple-system** (Apple Native Fallback)

### Primary Arabic Font Stack
- **Noto Sans Arabic** (Modern, readable for UI)
- **Tajawal** (Elegant, used for headings & marketing)
- **Noto Kufi Arabic** (Structured, geometric alternative)

## 2. Web Development (React / Next.js / HTML)

### Connecting via Google Fonts (CDN)
For standard web projects, place this in the `<head>` of your HTML or `_document.tsx` / `layout.tsx`:

```html
<link rel="preconnect" href="https://fonts.googleapis.com">
<link rel="preconnect" href="https://fonts.gstatic.com" crossorigin>
<link href="https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Inter:wght@400;500;700;900&family=Noto+Kufi+Arabic:wght@100..900&family=Noto+Sans+Arabic:wght@100..900&family=Tajawal:wght@200;300;400;500;700;800;900&display=swap" rel="stylesheet">
```

### CSS Implementation
```css
/* English Primary */
.font-inter {
  font-family: 'Inter', -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, 'Segoe UI', Roboto, sans-serif;
}

/* Arabic Modern UI */
.noto-sans-arabic {
  font-family: "Noto Sans Arabic", sans-serif;
  font-optical-sizing: auto;
  font-style: normal;
  font-variation-settings: "wdth" 100;
}

/* Arabic Headings */
.tajawal-bold {
  font-family: "Tajawal", sans-serif;
  font-weight: 700;
  font-style: normal;
}
```

### Next.js Native Optimization (Recommended for Performance)
In Next.js, use `next/font/google` instead of the CDN for zero layout shift:
```tsx
import { Inter, Noto_Sans_Arabic, Tajawal } from 'next/font/google'

const inter = Inter({ subsets: ['latin'], variable: '--font-inter' })
const notoSansArabic = Noto_Sans_Arabic({ subsets: ['arabic'], variable: '--font-noto-arabic' })
const tajawal = Tajawal({ weight: ['400', '700', '900'], subsets: ['arabic'], variable: '--font-tajawal' })
```

## 3. Mobile Development (Flutter)

Apps built with Flutter can directly access almost all fonts from Google Fonts through the `google_fonts` package.

**1. Add Dependency (`pubspec.yaml`):**
```yaml
dependencies:
  google_fonts: ^6.1.0
```

**2. Import the Package:**
```dart
import 'package:google_fonts/google_fonts.dart';
```

**3. Global Theme Application:**
```dart
ThemeData(
  // For Arabic UI
  textTheme: GoogleFonts.notoSansArabicTextTheme(),
)
// OR
ThemeData(
  // For English UI
  textTheme: GoogleFonts.interTextTheme(),
)
```

**4. Single Widget Application:**
```dart
Text(
  'مرحبا بكم في سمارت موتور',
  style: GoogleFonts.tajawal(
    fontWeight: FontWeight.w700,
    fontSize: 24,
  ),
),
```

### Advanced: Flutter Variable Fonts (Local Assets)
If you download the fonts locally to your `/assets/fonts/` directory, you can configure variable weights precisely:

```dart
TextStyle(
  fontFamily: 'Noto Sans Arabic',
  fontSize: 18,
  fontVariations: [
    FontVariation('ital', 0),
    FontVariation('wdth', 100),
    FontVariation('wght', 700) // 100 to 900
  ],
)
```

## 4. Offline / Edge Generation (e.g. Open Graph Images)
When generating graphics on edge servers (like our `api/og` route), you cannot use a CDN. You must:
1. Store the `.ttf` files locally in your repository (e.g., `/public/fonts/`).
2. Read the file into an ArrayBuffer.
3. Pass the raw font data into the rendering engine.
